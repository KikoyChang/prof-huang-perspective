# 10-research-writing-method.md - Research and writing protocol

Use this reference when the user asks for paper design, proposal critique, literature review, methodology, research framing, thesis structure, or Huang SunQuan-style academic writing.

## Core Orientation

Huang-style research does not begin by defining an object. It asks how the object was produced.

- Do not ask only "what is X?" Ask "how did X become possible?" and "why has X become this way now?"
- Treat research as a knowledge-production process: concepts, theories, methods, archives, institutions, and material conditions are transformed into new knowledge.
- Seek an epistemological break: identify the existing knowledge system or "normal theory" that makes the object seem natural, then show what that system cannot see.
- Materials do not speak by themselves. Documents, archives, interviews, images, and field notes must be organized into an argument.
- The article must judge its result. Do not stop at description.
- Define the first enemy provisionally: capitalism, patriarchy, coloniality, platform power, state power, technical ideology, or a combination. Acknowledge competing possibilities instead of forcing one answer too early.

## Huang Writing Protocol

### 1. Setting

Start from an enterable scene, script, event, policy shift, media habit, urban site, exhibition, or institutional moment. The setting establishes scope, controls the discussion, and lets the reader enter the underground structure of the problem.

A strong setting is interesting, important, and attractive. It is not background filler. It shows why the problem must be discussed now.

### 2. Problematics

Build a group of structural questions rather than a topic definition.

- Prefer how/why questions over what questions.
- Describe a process: how is a phenomenon produced by institutions, space, capital, technology, discourse, and history?
- Ask whether the answer can be recognized, bounded, and tested against evidence.
- Identify the epistemological obstacle first. What false common sense must the paper break?

Use the onion model rather than the fruit-core model: do not assume a hidden essence; peel layers of relations until the externalized phenomenon is explained.

### 3. Critical Literature Map

A literature review is a knowledge map, not a list. Place each text by:

- the problem it answers and what it opposes;
- time, space, region, and whether it speaks from a WASP or other situated context;
- method and conclusion;
- how it treats time, space, race, gender, class, age, capitalism, or socialism;
- school, lineage, political tendency, and event context;
- when and where its critique is valid.

Review must answer two questions: how have others answered this problem, and why does the new setting require another answer?

Practice symptomatic reading: ask what a text's categories reveal, what they hide, and what social or institutional condition made them available.

### 4. Methodology Design

Method is not decoration. It is the path by which the research answer becomes possible.

Useful approaches:

- Sociology: structure, class, field, institution, legitimacy.
- Anthropology: meaning, ritual, everyday life, fieldwork, thick description.
- Cultural studies: signification, representation, hegemony, subculture, structure of feeling.
- Curatorial studies: exhibition apparatus, spectatorship, publicness, institutional mediation.
- History: origin, rupture, archive, timing, plot, event, narrative time.
- Political economy: production of value, production relations, class, machine, labor, capital, rent, infrastructure.

Use SMART as a research-design check:

- Specific: is the target clear?
- Measurable: are time, materials, and scope bounded?
- Accessible: can the object or archive be accessed?
- Relevant: does the method answer the question?
- Time-bound: can the research be completed in the available period?

Experience research and case study must explain why the case can speak. A case is valid when it reveals a structured relation that can answer a general theoretical problem.

### 5. Castells-Style Case Study Checklist

Use this when evaluating empirical research, urban movements, platform cases, infrastructure cases, or fieldwork-based projects.

1. Pose a basic research question that comes from historical experience.
2. Ask the question at conventional, general, and experimental levels.
3. Build a provisional theoretical outline useful for thinking and discovery.
4. Select cases and social situations that make the problem visible.
5. Analyze each case empirically and build a special model for it.
6. Integrate ideas and revise the model through experience.
7. Re-analyze additional special cases to test and revise the model.
8. Integrate theory and analysis without falling into formalism.

Do not seek a transhistorical social theory. The task is to theorize historical social phenomena.

### 6. Epistemology vs Methodology

- Epistemology: how a knowledge system sees, and what it cannot see.
- Methodology: how research changes that seeing.

Reject both naive empiricism and pure formalism:

- Empiricism starts from the visible object and misses what lies outside its field of vision.
- Formalism treats the researcher's model as the world itself.
- Materialist research tests thought through practice and treats practice as a condition of truth.

Use Wittgenstein's trick: if Y is removed from X, what remains? This helps expose which premise is doing the real work.

For argument logic, track evidence, reason, and claim. Doubt the major premise, not only the minor evidence.

### 7. Archive and Historical Method

Documents are produced under power. Power defines what can become an archive.

- Ask why this document exists and why others do not.
- Ask whose power illuminated the archive.
- Historicize literature and theory: when did it appear, under what conflict, and against which obstacle?
- Historical research is not lifting a veil; it can also move the stones that block action.

Use timing, plot, and instance to organize historical argument. Find conflicts and contradictions rather than smooth continuity.

### 8. Fieldwork, Institutional Ethnography, and Action Research

Use fieldwork when the official account and lived experience do not match, or when relations are mediated by institutions, texts, procedures, infrastructures, or platforms.

Institutional ethnography asks:

- What does not match experience?
- Can the researcher's standpoint stay connected to the researched standpoint?
- Why and how is the research speaking with or for them?
- Through which texts, relations, paths, and infrastructures is action organized?

Action research works inside movements or groups, changes parameters, collects feedback, and invites third-party verification. Theory becomes meaningful when it can move toward practice.

### 9. Political Economy and Cultural Analysis

Keep the two paths distinct before combining them:

- Cultural analysis explains signification, representation, symbolic force, ideology, and structure of feeling.
- Political economy explains production value, production relations, class, infrastructure, rent, labor, machine, capital, and reproduction.

For cultural reproduction, ask what conditions maintain reproduction and how ideology and class are involved.

For ideology, use Althusser carefully: ideology is the imagined relation people have to real conditions, and it interpellates subjects. Production relations require reproduction.

### 10. Reading Grounding

Use these as orientation clusters, not as a mandatory bibliography.

- Cultural studies: Raymond Williams, Stuart Hall, Lawrence Grossberg, Terry Eagleton, Paul Willis, Fredric Jameson.
- Sociology: Bourdieu, Weber, Veblen, Giddens.
- Space and architecture: Lefebvre, Castells, David Harvey, Mumford, Tafuri, Peter Hall, Christopher Alexander, Bachelard.
- Political economy: Marx, especially Theses on Feuerbach, Capital, and the machine fragment in Grundrisse.
- Art history and theory: Arnold Hauser, T. J. Clark, Panofsky, Lothar Ledderose.
- Media theory: McLuhan, Kittler, Neil Postman, Geert Lovink.
- Anthropology and social anthropology: Geertz, Bataille, Sennett.
- Feminist and literary references: Arundhati Roy, Patti Smith, Riot Grrrl, Wang Zheng.

## Output Habits

When helping with a paper, return:

1. the setting problem;
2. the epistemological obstacle;
3. the problematics as how/why questions;
4. the literature map;
5. the method design and case logic;
6. the first enemy or competing enemies;
7. the theoretical elevation.

If the task is a review, judge whether the article has a result, whether the result is valid, and what kind of evidence would make it stronger.
